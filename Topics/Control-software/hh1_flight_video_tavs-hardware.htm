Hardware and Data Routing for TAVS Video

See Also for links to information about video functions and devices for TAVS.

This information applies only to ROV systems that have TAVS rather than HDEVS.

In TAVS, video streams from system cameras are sent topside, where the control system can display them on the monitor wall. You can also direct video streams to user-accessible ports for export to devices for recording or display.

For information on video collected from a survey junction box (optional), see the section on the survey junction box in Drawings and Part Lists.

Note:  In descriptions of TAVS signal routing, video signals are described as inputs or outputs from the viewpoint of the video switcher. For example, the output from a camera is described as an input because the signals are input to the video switcher.

TAVS Hardware Items

Video Interface Modules and DTS Nodes

The server (subsea) video interface module, or VIM (see Device Signal Types) takes composite video, digitizes and compresses it using the Motion JPEG format, and forwards it to the subsea node in the form of Ethernet packets. The subsea node receives the Ethernet packets and transports them through a fiber link to the topside rack nodes.

The client (topside) VIM receives the Ethernet packets through a fiber link, decodes the Motion JPEG, and creates a composite video output that is directed to the video switcher.

Video Switcher

The switcher receives incoming composite video streams and distributes them to one or more composite video outputs, as selected on the VIDEO SWITCH screen. A single video feed can be simultaneously routed to multiple locations. Switcher outputs are typically directed to an annotator and to the multiviewers that furnish video to the top-left and top-right screens on the monitor wall. Outputs can also be directed to user-supplied displays or recording equipment.

Annotator

The annotator allows the overlay of computer graphics, logos, arrows, pointers, and text onto three composite video images from the switcher. You can use the KVM-switched keyboard and mouse to access the annotator user interface. Through this interface, you can add new annotation text and graphics.

An annotator string, which is output from the serial expander, supplies ROV navigation data to the annotator serial port (see Annotator String Output for Video in TAVS).

For details on using the annotator, see the annotator OEM manual in Support Files\OEM Docs\Video\Video Annotators-Overlay\.

Video Multiviewers

Each multiviewer receives four video outputs and routes each to a quadrant on the top-left or top-right screen on the monitor wall (see About the Control Console Monitors).

TAVS Signal Routing/Configuration for Hardware and Software

To route and configure video signals properly, you must understand how signals travel through the DTS system and how the video switcher is used to route video inputs and outputs.

Note:  In descriptions of TAVS signal routing, video signals are described as inputs or outputs from the viewpoint of the video switcher. For example, the output from a camera is described as an input because the signals are input to the video switcher.

DTS Signal Routing

DTS signal routing is explained in detail in other sections of the technical manual. Concerning subsea cameras, note that camera signals are not processed through the ROV control system. Instead, a subsea camera is attached to a subsea node VIM port and signals are then tunneled to a rack node VIM. From the rack node VIM, signals are routed to the video switcher.

Video Switcher Routing

The video switcher contains input ports and output ports. These ports are connected with cables to video inputs (from devices like topside cameras) and outputs (to devices like monitors, annotators, and DVD recorders). Once cables have been connected to the video switcher, you must inform the control system about the hardware connections so that the control system can accurately route video. You do this by configuring the video switcher.

Note:  Cameras can be only video inputs to the switcher, and monitors can be only video outputs. Other devices, such as annotators and recorders, can simultaneously be both video inputs and outputs. See the ROV system interconnect drawing (in Drawings and Part Lists) to determine how video devices are connected for this ROV system.

If you do not accurately identify the hardware connections for the video switcher, video will not be correctly routed.
